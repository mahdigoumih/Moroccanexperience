About Casablanca

Where the Atlantic meets ambition

Morocco's economic powerhouse pulses with energy, ambition, and style. Casablanca is where Art Deco architecture meets rooftop bars, where the world's second-largest mosque rises dramatically from the Atlantic, and where tradition and modernity negotiate a fascinating coexistence.

A city of contrasts and surprises — the medina, the Habous quarter, Mohammed V Square, and the Arab League Park all tell different chapters of a city that has always looked both east and west.

Plan Your Visit
Stop 01

Hassan II Mosque

The world's third-largest mosque and one of the few open to non-Muslim visitors. Built on a promontory over the Atlantic, it features intricate zellige tilework, a retractable roof, a laser beam pointing toward Mecca, and the tallest minaret in the world at 210 meters.

Stop 02

Mohammed V Square

The administrative heart of Casablanca — a Mauresque masterpiece framed by the Palace of Justice, Prefecture, and Bank Al-Maghrib. The symmetrical gardens, fountains, and iconic clock tower make it the city's most photographed landmark.

Stop 03

Arab League Park

The green heart of Casablanca — a formal French-style park of palms, fountains, and tranquil alleys. It is framed by Notre Dame de Lourdes and the Sacré-Coeur, two remarkable Art Deco churches that stand as testament to Casablanca's layered colonial history.

Stop 04

Habous Quarter & Anfa

The Habous (New Medina) is a planned 1930s quarter blending traditional Moroccan architecture with French colonial planning — a unique hybrid that produced beautiful arcaded streets, a royal palace, and the finest traditional bakeries in the city. Nearby Anfa is where Casablanca began.
